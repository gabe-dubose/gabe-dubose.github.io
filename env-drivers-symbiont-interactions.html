 How do environmental facotrs influence
         host-symbiont interactions?


       The spcific state of a symbiotic interaction at any given point, as well at its trajectory
       over time can be variable. Commonly explored sources of this variability tend to focus on
       genetic factors that contribute to conflict or resolution between host and symbiont. However,
       environmental facotrs can also play a largr role in the stabilty and nature of a symbiosis,
       especially nascent symbioses. Further insight in the relative contribution of environmental
       factors towards symbiosis stability will help our overall understanding of shifts along
       the parasitism-mutualism spectrum. For example, a symbiont may have an initial fitness cost,
       but if the the host is not experiencing much stress from the environment, it might presist.


       This work is still on going under the direction of Dr. Tammy Haselkorn, but we have an
       ongoing goal of identifing abiotic soil factors that correlate with the prevalence
       of Paraburkholderia and Chlamydiae symbionts in natural social amoeba
       populations. One hypothesis is that if either symbiont tends to be more parasitic, it will
       exhibit lower levels of prevalence in more harsh soil conditions if the amoeba is unable to
       overcome the additive fitness cost of both the environment and the symbiont. However, if is
       also possible that more mutualistic symbionts will be more prevalent in more harsh environments
       if they provide a fitness benefit to their host. More on this is in the works!
