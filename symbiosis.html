Primary Questions:
--
How do symbiotic relationships shift along the parasitism-mutualism spectrum?
How do microbes transition to an endosymbiotic lifestyle?
How do environmental factors influence host-symbiont interactions?
--
Background:
--
The word "symbiosis" is often associated with the word "mutualism", or, an interaciton that benefits both partities. However, symbiotic interactions occur on a spectrum that ranges from parasitism to mutualism, and the position of a symbiotic relationship on this spectrum is not static. Therefore, symbioses are context depentent, where a mutualist in one context can act as a parasite in another. I am broadly interested in the how environmental factors and host dynamics drive movement along the parasitism-mutualism spectrum, and in some cases, to obligate endosymbiosis.
--
Social amoebae as a model organism:
--
To study shifts along the symbiotic spectrum, I use social ameoba and their various bacterial symbionts as a model system. My undergradate thesis work focused on understanding the ecological relevance of social amoeba symbionts by examing their genetic diveristy, transmission patterns, and temporal changes in prevalence in natural ameoba populations. D. discoideum, the primary model social amoeba, is well studied in the lab environment. However, the specific functions of their symbionts in a natural context are less established. Therefore, my undergraduate thesis work also focused on examining the bacterial microbiota harbored by social amoeba, aiming to further understand the natural relevance of their symbionts and microbiomes. As a graduate student, I have continued my work on social amoeba through collaboration with the Haselkorn lab at the University of Central Arkansas. We are currently working on understanding how abiotic factors influence symbiont dynamics in social amoeba populations and on examining the potential co-evolutionary history between amoeba and certain symbionts.
