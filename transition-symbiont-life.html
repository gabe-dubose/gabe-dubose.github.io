      How do microbes transition to an
             endosymbiotic lifestyle?


       One of the major transitions in evolution was the transition to endosymbiotic life. The classic
       example of this is the origins of mitochondria and chloroplast, where an initial endosymbiosis
       gave rise to the development of a new organelle. Although this was a very important transition,
       it occurred millions of years ago, making it diffucult to study how this process initiates and
       and progresses. However, studying nascent endosymbiotic interactions, we may be able to
       glean insight into how this process initially occurred (and even still occurs to this day). One key
       aspect to this transition is transmission. Here, microbes that are transmitted vertically form
       tighter associations with their hosts, thus intertwining the fitness of both organisms.


       My specific work focused on identifying the transmission patterns of various bacterial symbionts
       of D.discoideum. We first aimed to identified the genetic diversity of these symbionts.
       Generally, vertically transmitted symbionts undergo a genetic bottle neck upon transmission.
       This results in 1 or 2 haplotypes sweeping through populations. Alternatively, symbionts that
       are horizontally transmitted tend to remain genetically diverse, as they can diversify outside
       over their host. We then aimed to identify if symbionts of the same haplotypes were present in
       multiple social amoeba species. If a symbiont is vertically transmitted, we expected a single
       (or a few) haplotypes distinct to each species of social amoeba.

How diverse is Paraburkholderia in D.discoideum populations
and is there haplotype specificity between amoeba species?


       To look for signs that P. agricolaris (the most prevalent D.discoideum farming symbiont)
       was either vertically or horizontally transmitted (environmentally acquired), we examined its
       diveristy in in natural amoeba populations. Overall, we saw a high degree of diversity, including
       at least 10 haplotypes in D. discoidem, 7 of which had not been previously identified.
       We then screened other Dictyostelium species (D.mucoroides,D. giganteum,
       and D. purpureum), as well as other amoebae of different genera (Heterostelium pallidum
       and Polysphondylium pallidum). We found that several P.agricolaris haplotypes infected
       several different amoebae species and genera. The high degree of diversity, as well as the
       lack of haplotype specificity indicate that P. agricolaris can be environmentally acquired
       from nature. This was an interesting find because Paraburkholderia is also vertically transmitted.
       Perhaps this indicated an evolutionary intermediate along the transition to endosymbiosis.

Do other bacteria show signs of presistent associations with
D. discoideum?


       Several symbionts have been recorded as highly prevalent in natural social amoeba populations
       These include the members of Paraburkholderia, Chlamydiae, and Amoebophilus genera.
       However, due the close interactions amoebae have with bacteria, there is ample opprotunity,
       or rather a selective pressure, for bacteria to figure out how to evade amoeba predation and
       transition to an endosymbiotic life. Therefore, I am seeking to identify other bacteria that form
       a presistent association with D. discoideum by examining the whole bacterial microbiota.
       There has been some indication of a few other bacteria that are able to presist after passaging,
       a process that involves culturing amoebae and then culturing a fresh set from spores collected
       from said initial cultures. This process greatly reduces the overall microbiota diversity, leaving
       just a few members left that are significantly abundant. Future sequencing and culturing efforts
       may lend insight into the identification and relevance of these bacteria.
