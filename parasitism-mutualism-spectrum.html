How do symbiotic relationships shift along
     the parasitism-mutualism spectrum?


       Symbiotic interactions occur on a spectrum that ranges from parasitism to mutualism. When
       a symbiont encounters a new host, the initial interaction is typically parasitic in nature
       because parasites tend to employ mechanisms that allow it evade the host immune system. In
       the context of the transition to endosymbiosis, the symbiont must also employ mechanisms to
       evade intracellular digestion by the host. Although there is typically a cost of infection,
       if a mutual benefit to both organisms yields a fitness increase that can overcome the costs of
       infection, the interaction trends towards mutualism. However, this interaction is dynamic and a
       variety of factors can cause a symbiosis to shift along this spectrum.


       My specific work focused on trying to better understand the context dependency of symbiotic
       interaction between D. discoideum and its Paraburkholderia symbionts. Here, several
       Paraburkholderia symbionts allow their amoeba host to carry acquired food bacteria with them
       upon spore dispersal. This is beneficial to the amoeba if their spores disperse to an area
       depleted in local food bacteria. However, in food rich conditions, harboring these
       Paraburkholderia symbionts elicist a large fitness decrease in the amoeba. This was a well
       documented phenomenon in the lab, so I sought to look for evidence of where this interaction in
       tened to lie on the symbiotic spectrum in nature.

How does the prevalence of Paraburkholderia and other vary
temporally and spatially in natural amoeba populations?


       To first look at this symbiosis in a natural conext, I identified the prevalence of a several
       symbionts in a population that had been sampled 20 years prior. We reasoned that if a symbiont
       is behaving in a more mutualistic capacity (thus granting a fitness increase to its host),
       it would increase in prevalence over time. Likewise, we expected symbionts that were generally
       parasitic to decrease in prevalence if they were too detrimental. There are three
       Paraburkholderia symbionts that confer farming: P. agricolaris, P. hayleyella,
       and P. bonniea. Novel Chlamydiae symbionts have also been identified,
       but their role is less known. Overall, we found that P.hayleyella and P. bonniea
       had practically vanished from this natural population, and that P. agricolaris prevalence
       remained highly prevalent. We also found a high prevalence of Chlamydiae, which merits
       further investigation into its interaciton with D. discoideum.

How does the Paraburkholderia infection impact the D. discoidem
microbiome?


       Since the proposed benefit of Paraburkholderia infection is that it somehow allows
       D.discoideum to carry food bacteria with it, we reasoned that this would result in noticeable
       a noticeable change in the D. discoideum microbiome. This change could manifest in
       a few ways. First, D. discoideum infected with Paraburkholderia may harbor more
       diverse microbiota if Paraburkholderia randomly facilitate the carriage of bacteria from nature.
       Alternatively, infect amoeba may have more compositionally distinct (similar) microbiota,
       indicative of specific bacteria being carried from the environment. Overall, we did observe
       compositional similarities between infected amoebae. However, this similarity was attributed
       to the sheer abundance of Paraburkholderia present in the microbiota (usually >90% abundant).
       We also saw that Paraburkholeria corresponded with a general decrease in microbiota
       diversity. Overall, our findings change our understanding of how farming may occur in nature.
